# Physics, Chemistry, and Earth Science

## Gravity and Motion

Gravity is a fundamental force that attracts objects with mass toward each other. On Earth, all objects fall toward the ground because Earth's mass creates a gravitational field. The acceleration due to gravity at Earth's surface is approximately 9.8 m/s² (32 ft/s²). Isaac Newton described gravity with the inverse-square law: F = Gm₁m₂/r², where G is the gravitational constant (6.674×10⁻¹¹ N·m²/kg²). Objects fall toward Earth because Earth's mass is vastly greater than any ordinary object — the gravitational attraction is dominated by Earth's side of the equation.

Newton's first law of motion (inertia): An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion, unless acted upon by an external force.
Newton's second law: Force equals mass times acceleration (F = ma).
Newton's third law: Every action has an equal and opposite reaction.

Terminal velocity is the maximum speed a falling object reaches when air resistance equals gravitational force. A skydiver reaches about 195 km/h (120 mph) in spread-eagle position.

## Electricity and Conductors

Electric conductors allow electric current (the flow of electrons) to pass through them easily. Copper is one of the best electrical conductor metals because it has a single valence electron in its outer shell that is loosely bound and can move freely between atoms, forming a "sea of free electrons." This free electron mobility gives copper very low electrical resistance. Copper is a metal, as are other good conductors: silver (best conductor metal, but expensive), gold (resists corrosion), and aluminium.

Insulators (wood, rubber, glass, plastic) have electrons tightly bound to atoms — current cannot flow freely. Semiconductors (silicon, germanium) sit between conductors and insulators and can be tuned by adding impurities (doping).

Ohm's Law: V = IR (voltage = current × resistance). Resistance is measured in Ohms (Ω), current in Amperes (A), voltage in Volts (V).

## Light, Optics, and Atmospheric Phenomena

Refraction is the bending of light when it passes between media of different densities (e.g., air to glass or air to water). The refractive index determines how much light bends.

Rainbows form when sunlight enters water droplets, refracts (bends), reflects off the back of the droplet, then refracts again as it exits. Different wavelengths of light bend at different angles — red (longest wavelength) bends least, violet (shortest) bends most — producing the spectrum of colours: red, orange, yellow, green, blue, indigo, violet (ROYGBIV).

Atmospheric refraction causes stars to appear to twinkle (stellar scintillation). As starlight passes through Earth's atmosphere, varying pockets of air with different temperatures and densities continuously bend the light in slightly different directions, causing the apparent position and brightness to flicker rapidly. Stars near the horizon twinkle more because their light travels through more atmosphere. Planets twinkle less because they subtend a tiny disk (not a point source), averaging out the fluctuations.

The sky appears blue because of Rayleigh scattering: shorter-wavelength blue light is scattered more by atmospheric molecules than longer-wavelength red light. At sunset, light travels through more atmosphere, scattering away blue light and leaving red/orange hues.

Total internal reflection: when light hits a boundary at an angle greater than the critical angle, it reflects entirely back — this is how fibre optic cables work.

## Thermodynamics and States of Matter

The three laws of thermodynamics:
1. Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only converted (conservation of energy).
2. Entropy (disorder) of an isolated system always increases over time.
3. Absolute zero (0 K = −273.15°C) is the theoretical temperature at which all thermal motion stops.

The boiling point of water at sea level (1 atm pressure) is 100°C — one hundred degrees Celsius (212°F / 373.15 K). The boiling point is the temperature at which water transitions from liquid to gas. At higher altitudes, lower air pressure reduces the boiling point — water boils at ~90°C at 3,000 m elevation.
The melting/freezing point of water at standard pressure is 0°C (32°F).

Water is unusual: it is less dense as a solid (ice) than as a liquid. This is because water molecules form a hexagonal crystalline lattice when freezing, which takes up more space than the disordered liquid arrangement. Ice floats on water because its density (~0.92 g/cm³) is less than liquid water (~1.00 g/cm³). This property is vital for aquatic life — lakes freeze from the top down, leaving liquid water below.

Evaporation occurs when molecules at a liquid's surface gain enough kinetic energy to escape into the gas phase. This is a cooling process because the most energetic molecules leave (sweating cools the body through evaporative cooling).

## Chemistry and the Periodic Table

The periodic table organises elements by atomic number (number of protons) and groups elements with similar chemical properties in the same column (group).

Chemical symbols and elements — Au, Ag, Fe, Cu, Na, K, Pb, Hg:
- Au = Gold (from Latin "aurum"). Gold is element 79, a noble metal. The chemical symbol for gold is Au.
- Ag = Silver (from Latin "argentum"). Silver is element 47, the best electrical conductor.
- Fe = Iron (from Latin "ferrum"). Element 26, the most abundant element in Earth's core.
- Cu = Copper (from Latin "cuprum"). Element 29, excellent conductor.
- Na = Sodium (from Latin "natrium"). Element 11, a highly reactive alkali metal.
- K = Potassium (from Latin "kalium"). Element 19, essential for nerve function.
- Pb = Lead (from Latin "plumbum"). Element 82, very dense, toxic.
- Hg = Mercury (from Latin "hydrargyrum"). Element 80, the only liquid metal at room temperature.
- H = Hydrogen. Element 1, lightest and most abundant in the universe.
- He = Helium. Element 2, noble gas, second most abundant in the universe.
- O = Oxygen. Element 8, essential for respiration and combustion.
- C = Carbon. Element 6, the basis of all organic life.
- N = Nitrogen. Element 7, makes up 78% of Earth's atmosphere.

## Element Gold — Chemical Symbol Au

The element gold has the chemical symbol Au, derived from the Latin word "aurum." Gold is atomic number 79 on the periodic table. It is a noble metal that resists corrosion and oxidation, making it valuable for jewellery and electronics. The symbol Au is one of many chemical symbols derived from Latin: Ag = silver (argentum), Fe = iron (ferrum), Cu = copper (cuprum).

The Maillard reaction is a chemical reaction between amino acids and reducing sugars that produces brown colour and complex flavours when food is heated above ~140°C (284°F). It is responsible for the crust on bread, the browning of meat, the colour of beer, and the flavour of roasted coffee.

Oxidation-reduction (redox) reactions involve transfer of electrons. Oxidation = loss of electrons; Reduction = gain of electrons (OIL RIG mnemonic: Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain).

## Magnetism

Magnetism arises from the motion of electric charges (electrons). Ferromagnetic materials (iron, nickel, cobalt) can be magnetised because their electrons have unpaired spins that align into magnetic domains. When domains align in the same direction, the material becomes a magnet. Temperature destroys magnetism: heating iron above the Curie temperature (~770°C) randomises electron spins, eliminating ferromagnetism.

An electromagnet is created by running electric current through a coil of wire, which generates a magnetic field (Ampere's law). The Earth's magnetic field is generated by convection currents of molten iron in the outer core.

## Waves and Sound

Sound is a longitudinal mechanical wave — compressions and rarefactions of a medium (usually air). Sound travels at ~343 m/s in air at 20°C, ~1,480 m/s in water, ~5,120 m/s in steel.

The Doppler effect: the perceived frequency of a wave changes when the source and observer are moving relative to each other. A fire truck siren sounds higher-pitched as it approaches (compressed waves) and lower-pitched as it recedes (stretched waves).

Frequency is measured in Hertz (Hz). Human hearing range: ~20 Hz to 20,000 Hz. Ultrasound: above 20,000 Hz (used in medical imaging). Infrasound: below 20 Hz.

## Astronomy

A black hole is a region of spacetime where gravity is so strong that nothing, not even light, can escape. They form when massive stars collapse under their own gravity after a supernova. The event horizon is the boundary beyond which nothing can return. Supermassive black holes (millions to billions of solar masses) sit at the centres of most large galaxies, including the Milky Way (Sagittarius A*).

The speed of light in a vacuum is exactly 299,792,458 m/s (~3×10⁸ m/s or ~186,000 miles per second). Nothing with mass can reach or exceed this speed. Light from the Sun takes about 8 minutes to reach Earth.

A light-year is the distance light travels in one year (~9.46×10¹² km / 5.88×10¹² miles). The nearest star to the Sun is Proxima Centauri at 4.24 light-years.

## Aerodynamics and Flight

Airplanes stay in the air through four forces: lift, thrust, drag, and weight (gravity).
- Lift is generated by wings (aerofoils). The curved upper surface of a wing causes air to move faster over the top than underneath (Bernoulli's principle), creating lower pressure above — this pressure difference generates upward lift. Newton's third law also contributes: wings deflect air downward, and the reaction force pushes the plane up.
- Thrust: provided by engines (jet or propeller), pushes the plane forward.
- Drag: air resistance opposing forward motion.
- Weight: gravity pulling downward.

For level flight: lift = weight, thrust = drag.

## Ocean Chemistry

The ocean is salty primarily because of the water cycle and river runoff. Rainwater dissolves minerals and salts from rocks on land; rivers carry these dissolved minerals (especially sodium and chloride ions) to the ocean. Unlike water, which evaporates from the ocean surface and returns as fresh rain, salts accumulate over billions of years. The average ocean salinity is about 3.5% (35 parts per thousand). Hydrothermal vents on the ocean floor also add minerals.

The Dead Sea is extraordinarily salty (~34% salinity — about 10× normal seawater) because it sits in a closed basin with no outflow: the Jordan River flows in, water evaporates in the hot dry climate, but salts remain and concentrate. Nothing lives in the Dead Sea except salt-tolerant bacteria — hence the name.
