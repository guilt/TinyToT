# Psychology and Cognitive Science

## Core Psychological Theories

**Behaviourism**: psychology should study only observable behaviour, not internal states. Pavlov (classical conditioning): pairing a neutral stimulus with an unconditioned stimulus creates a conditioned response — dogs salivate at a bell after bell + food pairings. Skinner (operant conditioning): behaviour is shaped by reinforcement (increases behaviour) and punishment (decreases behaviour). Positive reinforcement = adding reward; negative reinforcement = removing an unpleasant stimulus.

**Psychoanalysis (Freud)**: unconscious drives and early experiences shape personality and behaviour. The unconscious contains repressed desires and memories. Defence mechanisms: repression, denial, projection, rationalisation, displacement. Freud's structural model: id (primitive drives), ego (reality principle), superego (moral conscience).

**Humanistic psychology (Maslow, Rogers)**: humans are motivated toward growth and self-actualisation. Maslow's hierarchy of needs (from base to apex): physiological → safety → love/belonging → esteem → self-actualisation. Carl Rogers: unconditional positive regard and empathy are core to therapeutic change.

**Cognitive psychology**: studies mental processes — perception, memory, attention, language, reasoning, problem-solving. Against behaviourism's "black box" — the mind matters.

## Memory

Three stages of memory: encoding (taking in information) → storage (retaining it) → retrieval (accessing it).

Types of memory:
- Sensory memory: very brief (fractions of a second); registers raw input.
- Short-term (working) memory: ~7 ± 2 items (Miller's law) held for ~20–30 seconds without rehearsal. Used for active mental manipulation (doing arithmetic in your head).
- Long-term memory: essentially unlimited capacity.
  - Explicit/declarative: facts (semantic) and personal events (episodic).
  - Implicit/procedural: skills and habits (riding a bike, typing).

The hippocampus is critical for forming new declarative memories. HM (Henry Molaison), who had his hippocampus removed, could no longer form new memories but retained old ones — evidence for hippocampal role in memory consolidation.

Forgetting: Ebbinghaus forgetting curve — memories fade rapidly at first, then level off. Spaced repetition (reviewing information at increasing intervals) fights forgetting. Interference: proactive (old memories interfere with new) and retroactive (new memories interfere with old).

## Cognition and Thinking

Heuristics: mental shortcuts that enable fast, frugal thinking — usually work well but can lead to systematic errors (biases).

Key cognitive biases:
- Availability heuristic: judging probability by how easily examples come to mind (overestimate plane crashes after news coverage).
- Representativeness heuristic: judging by similarity to a prototype (ignoring base rates).
- Anchoring: over-relying on the first piece of information received.
- Confirmation bias: seeking/interpreting information that confirms existing beliefs.
- Dunning-Kruger effect: incompetent people overestimate their own competence; experts underestimate theirs relative to others.
- Sunk cost fallacy: continuing an activity because of past investment, regardless of future value.
- Framing effect: how information is presented affects decisions ("90% survival rate" vs "10% mortality rate" — same information, different choices).

Kahneman's dual-process theory (System 1 and System 2): System 1 is fast, automatic, intuitive, pattern-matching. System 2 is slow, deliberate, effortful, analytical. Most errors come from over-relying on System 1 when System 2 is needed.

## Developmental Psychology

Piaget's stages of cognitive development:
- Sensorimotor (0–2 years): learns through senses and actions; develops object permanence (objects exist even when out of sight).
- Pre-operational (2–7 years): symbolic thinking/language; egocentric; lacks conservation (same amount of water in a tall thin vs short wide glass — child says tall has more).
- Concrete operational (7–12 years): logical thinking about concrete objects; masters conservation.
- Formal operational (12+): abstract reasoning, hypothetical thinking.

Attachment theory (Bowlby, Ainsworth): early emotional bond between infant and caregiver is critical for social and emotional development. Ainsworth's Strange Situation: secure attachment (distressed when caregiver leaves, comforted on return); anxious/avoidant; anxious/ambivalent; disorganised.

Erikson's psychosocial stages: eight stages of development from infancy to old age, each with a crisis to resolve (e.g. trust vs mistrust in infancy; identity vs role confusion in adolescence).

Vygotsky: learning occurs in the Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD) — tasks too hard to do alone but achievable with guidance (scaffolding). Language and thought are intertwined; learning is fundamentally social.

## Personality and Social Psychology

The Big Five personality traits (OCEAN):
- Openness to experience: curiosity, creativity.
- Conscientiousness: organisation, discipline, goal-directedness.
- Extraversion: sociability, assertiveness, positive emotion.
- Agreeableness: cooperation, trust, empathy.
- Neuroticism: emotional instability, anxiety, moodiness.

Social influence:
- Conformity (Asch): people conform to group norms even when the group is clearly wrong — 75% of participants gave at least one obviously wrong answer when confederates did.
- Obedience (Milgram): 65% of participants administered what they thought were dangerous electric shocks when ordered by an authority figure.
- Bystander effect (Kitty Genovese case): as the number of bystanders increases, the probability any one person helps decreases — diffusion of responsibility.
- Fundamental attribution error: overestimating dispositional (personality) factors and underestimating situational factors when explaining others' behaviour.

## Motivation and Emotion

Intrinsic motivation: driven by internal rewards (curiosity, satisfaction, love of the activity). Extrinsic motivation: driven by external rewards (money, grades, praise). Over-justification effect: adding extrinsic rewards to an intrinsically motivating activity can undermine intrinsic motivation.

The James-Lange theory of emotion: physiological changes precede and cause emotional experience (we are afraid because we tremble, not the other way around). Cannon-Bard theory: physiological response and emotional experience occur simultaneously. Schachter-Singer two-factor theory: emotion = physiological arousal + cognitive label of that arousal.

## Research Methods in Psychology

Random assignment: participants are randomly assigned to experimental conditions — controls for confounding variables. Key to establishing causation (correlation ≠ causation).

Experimental group vs control group: the experimental group receives the treatment; the control group does not (or receives a placebo).

Double-blind study: neither participants nor experimenters know who is in which condition — controls for placebo effect and experimenter bias.

Reliability: consistency of measurement — same result on repeated testing. Validity: accuracy — does it measure what it claims to?

Sampling bias: when the sample doesn't represent the population. Publication bias: positive results are more likely to be published, inflating apparent effect sizes.
