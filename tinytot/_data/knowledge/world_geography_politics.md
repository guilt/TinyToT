## Largest and Most Populous Countries

By area (largest): Russia, Canada, United States, China, Brazil, Australia, India, Argentina, Kazakhstan, Algeria.

By population (most populous): India (surpassed China ~2023, ~1.43 billion), China (~1.41 billion), United States (~335 million), Indonesia, Pakistan, Brazil, Nigeria, Bangladesh, Russia, Ethiopia.

Most densely populated countries: Monaco, Singapore, Bahrain, Bangladesh, Taiwan.

Largest cities (metro areas, approximate): Tokyo (~37M), Delhi (~32M), Shanghai (~29M), São Paulo (~22M), Mumbai (~21M), Beijing (~21M), Cairo (~21M), New York (~19M), Dhaka (~22M), Karachi (~17M).

## Physical Geography

Major mountain ranges: Himalayas (highest, includes Everest at 8,849 m), Andes (longest, runs down western South America), Rockies (North America), Alps (Europe), Urals (divides Europe and Asia), Atlas (North Africa), Appalachians (eastern USA).

Major rivers: Nile (longest river, ~6,650 km, Africa; debated with Amazon), Amazon (largest by discharge, South America), Yangtze (longest in China), Mississippi-Missouri (USA), Congo (Africa), Ganges (India), Volga (longest in Europe), Rhine (Western Europe), Danube (Central/Eastern Europe).

Deserts: Sahara (largest hot desert, Africa, ~9.2 million km²), Arabian Desert, Gobi (Asia, cold desert), Great Victoria (Australia), Patagonian (South America, cold), Antarctic (largest desert overall, cold).

Largest oceans: Pacific > Atlantic > Indian > Southern > Arctic.

## Major International Organisations

United Nations (UN): 193 member states; promotes peace, security, human rights, and development. Key bodies: Security Council (5 permanent members with veto: US, UK, France, Russia, China), General Assembly, International Court of Justice, UNICEF, WHO (World Health Organization), UNESCO.

NATO (North Atlantic Treaty Organization): military alliance of primarily Western countries. Article 5: an attack on one member is an attack on all.

World Trade Organization (WTO): oversees international trade rules; settles trade disputes; promotes free trade.

International Monetary Fund (IMF): provides financial assistance and economic policy advice to countries facing balance-of-payments crises.

World Bank: provides loans and grants to developing countries for development projects.

European Union (EU): political and economic union of 27 European countries; single market; common currency (euro, used by 20 member states). EU institutions: European Commission (executive), European Parliament, Council of the EU, European Court of Justice.

G7: group of seven major advanced economies (US, UK, France, Germany, Italy, Japan, Canada + EU). G20: expands to include emerging economies (adds China, India, Brazil, Russia, etc.).

ASEAN (Association of Southeast Asian Nations): 10 members promoting economic integration in Southeast Asia.

African Union (AU): 55 member states promoting unity and development in Africa.

BRICS: Brazil, Russia, India, China, South Africa — expanded to include more countries (Saudi Arabia, UAE, Iran, Egypt, Ethiopia, Argentina invited in 2023).

## Key Geopolitical Concepts

State sovereignty: the principle that each state has supreme authority within its territory and no external power has the right to interfere.

Balance of power: the distribution of power among states — historically, states have balanced against dominant powers to prevent hegemony.

Globalisation: increasing economic, cultural, political, and technological interconnectedness worldwide — driven by trade liberalisation, communication technology, and multinational corporations.

Nuclear deterrence: the doctrine that possessing nuclear weapons deters attack because the costs would be catastrophic. Major nuclear powers: USA, Russia (~90% of global warheads), China, France, UK, India, Pakistan, North Korea, Israel (undeclared).

Climate diplomacy: international agreements to address climate change. Kyoto Protocol (1997): binding emissions targets for developed countries. Paris Agreement (2015): 196 parties commit to limiting global warming to 1.5–2°C above pre-industrial levels; nationally determined contributions.

## Political Systems

Democratic systems:
- Presidential (US): directly elected president serves as both head of state and government; strong separation of powers.
- Parliamentary (UK, Germany, India): Prime Minister is leader of the legislative majority; head of state is separate (monarch or president).
- Semi-presidential (France): directly elected president + prime minister accountable to parliament.

Non-democratic systems:
- Authoritarian: concentrated power, limited political freedoms; elections may exist but are not free and fair.
- Totalitarian: seeks to control all aspects of public and private life (e.g. Nazi Germany, Soviet Stalinism, North Korea).
- Theocracy: religious leaders hold political authority (Iran, Vatican City).

Electoral systems:
- First-past-the-post (FPTP): candidate with most votes wins, even without majority. Used in US, UK.
- Proportional representation (PR): seats allocated proportionally to vote share. Used in Germany, Netherlands.
- Mixed: combines elements of both (Germany's MMP — Mixed Member Proportional).
