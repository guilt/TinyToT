# Medicine, Anatomy, and Health Science

## The Cardiovascular System

The heart is a muscular pump that circulates blood through two circuits:
- Pulmonary circuit: right side of the heart pumps deoxygenated blood to the lungs, where it picks up oxygen and releases CO₂.
- Systemic circuit: left side of the heart pumps oxygenated blood to the rest of the body.

The heart has four chambers: right atrium (receives deoxygenated blood from the body), right ventricle (pumps it to lungs), left atrium (receives oxygenated blood from lungs), left ventricle (pumps it to the body). The left ventricle has the thickest wall because it must generate the most pressure.

Blood vessels: arteries carry blood away from the heart (usually oxygenated); veins carry blood toward the heart (usually deoxygenated). Capillaries are the tiny vessels where gas and nutrient exchange occurs.

Blood pressure is the force of blood against artery walls. Normal: ~120/80 mmHg (systolic/diastolic). Hypertension (high blood pressure): consistently ≥130/80; increases risk of heart attack and stroke.

## The Respiratory System

The primary function is gas exchange: bringing oxygen into the body and removing carbon dioxide. Air enters through the nose/mouth → trachea → bronchi → bronchioles → alveoli (tiny air sacs in the lungs). In the alveoli, oxygen diffuses into surrounding capillaries and CO₂ diffuses out.

The diaphragm is the dome-shaped muscle below the lungs — it contracts to inhale (lung volume increases, pressure drops, air rushes in) and relaxes to exhale.

Asthma: chronic inflammation and narrowing of airways — treated with bronchodilators (beta-agonists) and anti-inflammatory corticosteroids. COPD (Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease): progressive lung disease usually caused by smoking.

## The Immune System

Two branches of immunity:
- Innate immunity: non-specific, immediate response — skin barrier, mucus, fever, neutrophils and macrophages that engulf pathogens.
- Adaptive immunity: specific, learned response — takes days but creates immunological memory.

B cells produce antibodies (immunoglobulins) that bind specifically to antigens (foreign molecules). T cells: cytotoxic T cells kill infected cells directly; helper T cells coordinate the immune response. Memory B and T cells persist after infection, enabling rapid response if the same pathogen appears again.

Vaccines stimulate adaptive immunity without causing disease. Types: live-attenuated (MMR, chickenpox), inactivated (flu shot), subunit (hepatitis B), mRNA (some COVID-19 vaccines instruct cells to make a pathogen protein).

Autoimmune diseases occur when the immune system attacks the body's own cells: rheumatoid arthritis (joints), type 1 diabetes (pancreatic beta cells), multiple sclerosis (myelin sheaths), lupus.

## Common Diseases and Conditions

Diabetes: two main types.
- Type 1: autoimmune destruction of insulin-producing beta cells in the pancreas — requires insulin injections. Usually diagnosed in childhood.
- Type 2: insulin resistance — cells don't respond properly to insulin. Managed with diet, exercise, metformin, and/or insulin. Associated with obesity and sedentary lifestyle.

Insulin is a hormone produced by the pancreas that allows glucose to enter cells. Without insulin, glucose builds up in the blood (hyperglycaemia).

Heart attack (myocardial infarction): blockage of a coronary artery cuts off blood supply to part of the heart muscle — causes chest pain, shortness of breath, arm or jaw pain. Treatment: restore blood flow via thrombolytics or percutaneous coronary intervention (stent).

Stroke: interruption of blood flow to the brain. Ischaemic stroke (most common): blood clot blocks an artery. Haemorrhagic stroke: a blood vessel ruptures. Symptoms: sudden face drooping, arm weakness, speech difficulty (FAST acronym: Face, Arms, Speech, Time to call emergency services).

Cancer: uncontrolled cell division caused by mutations in DNA. Carcinogens (smoking, UV radiation, some viruses like HPV) and inherited mutations (BRCA1/2) increase risk. Treated by surgery, chemotherapy, radiation, immunotherapy, or targeted therapy.

## The Nervous System

Central nervous system (CNS): brain + spinal cord. Brain regions:
- Cerebral cortex: higher functions — thought, language, voluntary movement, sensory perception.
- Frontal lobe: decision-making, personality, speech (Broca's area).
- Temporal lobe: memory, hearing, language comprehension (Wernicke's area).
- Parietal lobe: spatial processing, touch, body position.
- Occipital lobe: vision.
- Cerebellum: coordination, balance, fine motor control.
- Brain stem (medulla, pons, midbrain): vital automatic functions — breathing, heart rate, blood pressure.
- Hippocampus: consolidation of new memories.
- Amygdala: emotional processing (fear, aggression).

Peripheral nervous system (PNS):
- Somatic: voluntary movement and sensory input.
- Autonomic: involuntary (heart, digestion, breathing).
  - Sympathetic ("fight or flight"): increases heart rate, dilates pupils, inhibits digestion.
  - Parasympathetic ("rest and digest"): decreases heart rate, promotes digestion, constricts pupils.

Neurotransmitters: chemical messengers at synapses. Dopamine: reward, motivation; low in Parkinson's disease. Serotonin: mood, sleep; low in depression. Acetylcholine: muscle contraction, memory; loss of cholinergic neurons occurs in Alzheimer's. GABA: main inhibitory neurotransmitter — benzodiazepines (Valium) enhance GABA.

## Pharmacology Basics

A drug's mechanism of action describes how it produces its effect:
- Agonist: binds to a receptor and activates it (e.g. morphine activates opioid receptors).
- Antagonist: binds to a receptor and blocks it (e.g. beta-blockers block beta-adrenergic receptors to slow the heart).

Antibiotics kill or inhibit bacteria: penicillins/cephalosporins disrupt cell wall synthesis; fluoroquinolones inhibit DNA replication; macrolides inhibit protein synthesis. Antibiotic resistance arises when bacteria evolve mechanisms to survive antibiotics — major global health concern.

Analgesics relieve pain: paracetamol/acetaminophen (inhibits prostaglandin synthesis in CNS), NSAIDs like ibuprofen (inhibit COX enzymes, reducing inflammation and pain), opioids like morphine/codeine (bind opioid receptors in CNS).

## Vitamins and Nutrition

Essential vitamins:
- Vitamin A: vision (retinal), immune function. Deficiency → night blindness.
- Vitamin B12: nerve function, red blood cell formation. Deficiency → megaloblastic anaemia, neuropathy.
- Vitamin C (ascorbic acid): collagen synthesis, antioxidant. Deficiency → scurvy.
- Vitamin D: calcium absorption, bone health. Deficiency → rickets (children), osteomalacia (adults). Synthesised from sunlight.
- Vitamin K: blood clotting. Deficiency → bleeding disorders.

Macronutrients: carbohydrates (4 kcal/g), proteins (4 kcal/g), fats (9 kcal/g). Essential amino acids cannot be synthesised by the body — must come from diet. Essential fatty acids: omega-3 and omega-6.

## Genetics and Heredity

Mendelian genetics: traits are determined by pairs of alleles (one from each parent). Dominant alleles mask recessive ones. A capital letter denotes dominant (B), lowercase recessive (b).

Genotype: the genetic makeup (BB, Bb, bb). Phenotype: the observable trait.
If B = brown eyes (dominant), b = blue eyes (recessive):
- BB = brown, Bb = brown, bb = blue.
- Two Bb parents: 3/4 chance brown, 1/4 chance blue (Punnett square).

Sex-linked traits are carried on the X chromosome. Colour blindness and haemophilia are X-linked recessive — more common in males (XY) because they have only one X.

Mutation: a change in DNA sequence. Point mutation: single base change. Chromosomal mutation: deletion, duplication, inversion, translocation of chromosome segments. Down syndrome: trisomy 21 (three copies of chromosome 21).

## Mental Health

Depression (major depressive disorder): persistent low mood, loss of interest, fatigue, sleep and appetite changes, feelings of worthlessness. Treated with cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT), antidepressants (SSRIs like fluoxetine increase serotonin availability).

Anxiety disorders: excessive worry, panic attacks, phobias. Treated with CBT, exposure therapy, SSRIs, benzodiazepines (short-term).

Schizophrenia: psychosis — hallucinations (hearing voices), delusions (false beliefs), disorganised thinking. Treated with antipsychotics (D2 receptor antagonists).

Bipolar disorder: episodes of mania (elevated mood, reduced sleep, grandiosity, impulsivity) alternating with depression. Treated with mood stabilisers (lithium, valproate).

PTSD (Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder): after trauma — flashbacks, nightmares, hypervigilance, emotional numbing. Treated with trauma-focused CBT, EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitisation and Reprocessing).
