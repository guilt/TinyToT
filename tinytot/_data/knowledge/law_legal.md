# Law and Legal Reasoning

## Foundations of Law

Law is a system of rules enforceable by social institutions to govern behaviour. Two main legal traditions:
- Common law: law develops through court decisions (precedent/case law). Used in UK, USA, Australia, Canada. Judges interpret statutes and prior rulings.
- Civil law: based on comprehensive written codes (statutes). Used in continental Europe, Latin America, Japan. Judges apply the code rather than developing law through precedent.

Precedent (stare decisis): courts are bound to follow earlier decisions on the same legal issue from higher courts in the same jurisdiction. Ensures consistency and predictability.

Jurisdiction: the authority of a court to hear a case — determined by geography, subject matter, and parties.

## Criminal Law

Criminal law governs offences against the state/society. Two elements required for criminal liability:
- Actus reus ("guilty act"): the physical act or omission.
- Mens rea ("guilty mind"): criminal intent or knowledge. Most crimes require both; strict liability offences (e.g. speeding) require only the act.

Types of criminal offences:
- Felonies (serious crimes — murder, rape, robbery): typically punishable by imprisonment >1 year.
- Misdemeanours (minor crimes — petty theft, minor assault): less severe penalties.

Standard of proof in criminal cases: "beyond reasonable doubt" — very high standard to protect the accused.

Key constitutional protections (US — 4th, 5th, 6th Amendments):
- 4th Amendment: protection against unreasonable searches and seizures; warrants required.
- 5th Amendment: right against self-incrimination; no double jeopardy; due process.
- 6th Amendment: right to speedy trial, impartial jury, confront witnesses, counsel.

Miranda rights (US): police must inform suspects of their right to remain silent and right to an attorney before custodial interrogation.

## Contract Law

A contract is a legally binding agreement. Elements required for a valid contract:
1. Offer: a definite proposal to enter an agreement.
2. Acceptance: unconditional agreement to the offer's terms.
3. Consideration: something of value exchanged by each party (money, goods, services, a promise).
4. Capacity: parties must have legal capacity (adults of sound mind).
5. Legality: the contract's purpose must be legal.

A contract can be written or oral (though some types — real estate, marriage — must be written).

Breach of contract: failure to fulfil contractual obligations without legal excuse. Remedies:
- Damages: monetary compensation (compensatory, consequential, nominal, punitive).
- Specific performance: court orders the breaching party to fulfil the contract (used for unique goods/real estate).
- Rescission: contract is cancelled; parties restored to original position.

Void contract: has no legal effect from the start (e.g. based on illegal activity). Voidable contract: valid but can be cancelled by one party (e.g. signed under duress or by a minor).

## Tort Law

A tort is a civil wrong that causes harm to another, entitling the victim to compensation. Torts are not crimes (though some acts are both).

Key torts:
- Negligence: failure to exercise reasonable care that causes harm. Elements: duty of care, breach of duty, causation, damages. Example: a driver who runs a red light and injures a pedestrian.
- Defamation: false statement of fact presented as true that damages someone's reputation. Libel = written; slander = spoken. Truth is an absolute defence.
- Nuisance: interference with another's use and enjoyment of land.
- Strict liability: liability without proof of fault — applies to abnormally dangerous activities and defective products.

Standard of proof in civil cases: "preponderance of the evidence" (more likely than not, >50%) — lower than criminal standard.

## Constitutional Law

A constitution is the supreme law of a country — it establishes government structure and protects fundamental rights. Parliamentary sovereignty (UK): Parliament can make or unmake any law; no written constitution. Judicial review (US): courts can strike down laws that violate the constitution.

The US Constitution:
- Separation of powers: legislative (Congress), executive (President), judicial (federal courts).
- Bill of Rights (first 10 amendments): free speech (1st), right to bear arms (2nd), due process (5th/14th), equal protection (14th).
- The 14th Amendment's equal protection clause underpins many civil rights decisions.

Rule of law: no one is above the law; laws are applied equally; government authority is constrained by law.

## Intellectual Property

- Copyright: protects original creative works (books, music, software, films) automatically from creation. Duration: life of author + 70 years (varies by jurisdiction). Gives exclusive rights to reproduce, distribute, perform, and create derivatives.
- Trademark: protects words, logos, symbols that identify a brand. Must be registered and actively used; can last indefinitely.
- Patent: protects inventions — grants exclusive rights for 20 years from filing. Requires novelty, non-obviousness, utility, and full disclosure.
- Trade secret: confidential business information (formulas, methods) — protected as long as kept secret (Coca-Cola formula, Google's search algorithm).

## Human Rights Law

The Universal Declaration of Human Rights (UDHR, 1948) established internationally recognised rights: life, liberty, security; freedom from torture; right to fair trial; freedom of expression, religion, assembly; right to education and work.

The European Convention on Human Rights (ECHR) is enforceable by the European Court of Human Rights. The International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights (ICCPR) and the Convention Against Torture are key UN treaties.

Habeas corpus: the right to challenge unlawful detention before a court — one of the oldest legal protections (English law since Magna Carta, 1215).
