# Earth Science, Astronomy, and Physical Geography

## Continents and Oceans

There are 7 continents on Earth: Africa, Antarctica, Asia, Australia (Oceania), Europe, North America, and South America. Asia is the largest continent by both area and population. Australia is the smallest continent. Antarctica is the only continent with no permanent human population.

There are 5 oceans: Pacific (largest), Atlantic, Indian, Southern (Antarctic), and Arctic (smallest). The Pacific Ocean is larger than all landmasses combined.

## The Solar System

There are 8 planets in the solar system (Pluto was reclassified as a dwarf planet in 2006): Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune. The four inner rocky planets are Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars. The four outer gas giants are Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune.

Jupiter is the largest planet. Mercury is the smallest and closest to the Sun. Venus is the hottest planet (due to its dense CO₂ atmosphere and greenhouse effect). Earth is the only planet known to support life.

The Sun is a medium-sized star at the centre of the solar system, composed mainly of hydrogen and helium. It generates energy through nuclear fusion.

Saturn is famous for its ring system made of ice and rock particles.

A light-year is the distance light travels in one year (~9.46×10¹² km). The nearest star to our Sun is Proxima Centauri, 4.24 light-years away.

## The Big Bang Theory

The Big Bang theory is the prevailing cosmological model for the origin of the universe. Approximately 13.8 billion years ago, all matter and energy in the universe was compressed into an extremely hot, dense point (a singularity), which rapidly expanded. As the universe expanded and cooled, subatomic particles formed, then atoms (mainly hydrogen and helium), then stars and galaxies coalesced under gravity. Evidence includes the cosmic microwave background radiation (leftover heat from the early universe) and the observed expansion of the universe (redshift of distant galaxies).

## Black Holes

A black hole is a region of spacetime where gravity is so strong that nothing — not even light — can escape past the event horizon. Black holes form when massive stars (at least 20× the Sun's mass) exhaust their nuclear fuel and collapse under their own gravity in a supernova explosion. Supermassive black holes (millions to billions of solar masses) exist at the centre of most large galaxies, including the Milky Way (Sagittarius A*, pronounced "A-star").

The event horizon is the boundary of no return — once matter crosses it, it cannot escape. Stephen Hawking proposed that black holes emit a faint thermal radiation (Hawking radiation) over astronomical timescales.

## Seasons and Earth's Tilt

The seasons are caused by the tilt of Earth's axis (approximately 23.5°), not by Earth's distance from the Sun. When the Northern Hemisphere is tilted toward the Sun (around June), it receives more direct sunlight and experiences summer. When tilted away (around December), it receives less direct sunlight — winter. The Southern Hemisphere has opposite seasons.

At the equinoxes (around March 20 and September 22), day and night are approximately equal in length worldwide. At the solstices (around June 21 and December 21), one hemisphere has its longest day and the other its shortest.

## The Water Cycle (Hydrological Cycle)

The water cycle describes the continuous movement of water through Earth's systems:
1. Evaporation: liquid water from oceans, lakes, and rivers is heated by the Sun and turns to water vapour.
2. Transpiration: plants release water vapour through their leaves.
3. Condensation: water vapour rises, cools, and condenses around dust particles to form clouds and fog.
4. Precipitation: water falls as rain, snow, sleet, or hail.
5. Collection: water flows into rivers, lakes, and groundwater aquifers, then back to the ocean.
6. Infiltration: some water seeps into the soil and becomes groundwater.

## Earthquakes and Plate Tectonics

Earthquakes are caused by the sudden release of energy stored in Earth's crust, usually at tectonic plate boundaries. Earth's lithosphere is divided into approximately 15 major tectonic plates that move slowly (a few centimetres per year) due to convection currents in the mantle.

Types of plate boundaries:
- Convergent: plates collide — one may subduct under the other, forming mountain ranges (Himalayas) or ocean trenches.
- Divergent: plates move apart — magma rises to fill the gap (mid-ocean ridges, East African Rift Valley).
- Transform: plates slide past each other horizontally — cause major earthquakes (San Andreas Fault).

The magnitude of an earthquake is measured on the Richter scale (logarithmic). Each integer step = 10× more ground motion amplitude. Volcanic eruptions are also linked to plate tectonics — most volcanoes occur at convergent or divergent boundaries (the "Ring of Fire" around the Pacific).

## Thunder, Lightning, and Storms

Lightning is a discharge of static electricity between clouds or between a cloud and the ground. Inside a thundercloud (cumulonimbus), updrafts separate electric charges — positive charge builds at the top, negative at the bottom. When the charge difference is large enough, lightning discharges to neutralise it. The sudden heating of air by lightning (to ~30,000 K, five times hotter than the Sun's surface) causes rapid expansion — this is thunder. Sound travels slower than light, so you hear thunder after seeing lightning. The delay in seconds ÷ 3 gives the approximate distance in kilometres.

## Climate Change and the Greenhouse Effect

The greenhouse effect: the Sun's radiation passes through the atmosphere and warms Earth's surface. The warmed surface emits infrared radiation (heat), which is absorbed by greenhouse gases (CO₂, methane, water vapour, nitrous oxide) in the atmosphere and re-radiated back toward Earth, keeping the planet warm. Without any greenhouse effect, Earth's average temperature would be about −18°C instead of +15°C.

Climate change refers to the long-term alteration of Earth's climate patterns, primarily caused by human activities burning fossil fuels (coal, oil, natural gas), which release CO₂ and other greenhouse gases. Since pre-industrial times, atmospheric CO₂ has risen from ~280 ppm to over 420 ppm (2024). Consequences include rising temperatures, melting glaciers and polar ice, sea level rise, more extreme weather events, and shifts in ecosystems.

## Osmosis and Diffusion

Osmosis is the movement of water molecules across a semi-permeable membrane from a region of lower solute concentration (higher water concentration) to a region of higher solute concentration (lower water concentration). It continues until concentrations are equal or until hydrostatic pressure prevents further flow. Example: plant roots absorb water from soil by osmosis; red blood cells swell in pure water (hypotonic solution) and shrink in saltwater (hypertonic solution).

Diffusion is the movement of molecules from high concentration to low concentration (down the concentration gradient) — no membrane required. Example: oxygen diffuses from lungs into blood; carbon dioxide diffuses from blood into lungs.

## The Atmosphere

Earth's atmosphere is layered:
- Troposphere (0–12 km): where weather occurs; contains 75% of the atmosphere's mass.
- Stratosphere (12–50 km): contains the ozone layer, which absorbs UV radiation.
- Mesosphere (50–80 km): where most meteors burn up.
- Thermosphere (80–600 km): where auroras occur.
- Exosphere (600 km+): gradually merges with outer space.

The ozone layer (O₃) absorbs harmful ultraviolet radiation from the Sun. CFC compounds (once used in refrigerants and aerosols) were found to deplete ozone, leading to the Montreal Protocol (1987) which phased them out.

## Evolution by Natural Selection

Evolution is the change in heritable traits in populations over successive generations. Charles Darwin and Alfred Russel Wallace independently proposed natural selection as the mechanism:
1. Variation: individuals within a population vary in their traits.
2. Inheritance: traits are passed from parents to offspring.
3. Selection pressure: some traits improve survival or reproduction in a given environment.
4. Differential reproduction: individuals with advantageous traits reproduce more, passing those traits on.
5. Over generations, advantageous traits become more common (the population "evolves").

Evidence for evolution includes the fossil record, comparative anatomy (homologous structures), DNA similarities, direct observation of evolution in fast-reproducing organisms (bacteria, viruses), and biogeography.
