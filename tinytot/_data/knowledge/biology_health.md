# Biology and Human Health

## Viruses vs Bacteria

Bacteria are single-celled living organisms with their own cellular machinery — they have a cell wall, cell membrane, ribosomes, and DNA floating freely in the cytoplasm (no nucleus — they are prokaryotes). Bacteria can reproduce on their own through binary fission. Examples: E. coli, Staphylococcus, Salmonella.

Viruses are not living cells. A virus is a strand of genetic material (DNA or RNA) wrapped in a protein coat (capsid). Viruses have no cells, no ribosomes, and cannot reproduce on their own — they must invade a host cell and hijack its machinery to replicate. Viruses are smaller than bacteria (typically 10–100× smaller). Examples: influenza, SARS-CoV-2, HIV.

Key difference: bacteria are living cells (prokaryotes); viruses are not cells — they are obligate intracellular parasites with DNA or RNA but no independent metabolism. Antibiotics kill bacteria but have no effect on viruses.

## The Human Digestive System

Digestion is the process of breaking down food into nutrients the body can absorb. The main organs involved:

1. Mouth — mechanical chewing, saliva starts carbohydrate digestion with amylase.
2. Oesophagus — muscular tube that pushes food to the stomach via peristalsis.
3. Stomach — churns food with hydrochloric acid (pH 1–2) and pepsin, breaking down proteins. Creates chyme.
4. Small intestine — primary site of digestion and nutrient absorption. Three parts: duodenum, jejunum, ileum. Bile (from the liver) and pancreatic enzymes break down fats, proteins, and carbohydrates. Nutrients are absorbed through finger-like projections called villi.
5. Large intestine (colon) — absorbs water and electrolytes; bacteria ferment undigested fibre.
6. Rectum and anus — store and expel waste (faeces).

The small intestine is where most digestion and absorption of nutrients occurs. The stomach digests proteins. The liver produces bile, which emulsifies fats.

## Skin: The Largest Organ

The skin is the largest organ of the human body by surface area (approximately 1.5–2 m²) and weight (about 4–5 kg in adults). The skin has three main layers:
- Epidermis (outermost) — barrier against pathogens, produces melanin.
- Dermis — contains hair follicles, sweat glands, nerve endings, blood vessels.
- Hypodermis (subcutaneous layer) — fat and connective tissue, insulation.

Functions of skin: protection (barrier against bacteria, UV radiation), thermoregulation (sweating, blood flow), sensation (touch, pain, temperature), vitamin D synthesis (from sunlight), waterproofing.

The largest organ in the human body is the skin — by surface area (approximately 1.5–2 m²) and weight (about 4–5 kg in adults). The skin is the body's largest organ, covering the entire body and serving as a barrier against infection. The human body has 206 bones in adults. The largest internal organ is the liver.

## DNA and Genetics

DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid) is the molecule that encodes genetic information. It is a double helix made of two complementary strands of nucleotides. The four bases are: Adenine (A) pairs with Thymine (T); Cytosine (C) pairs with Guanine (G).

A gene is a segment of DNA that encodes a protein. Humans have approximately 20,000–25,000 genes packed into 23 pairs of chromosomes.

RNA (ribonucleic acid) carries messages from DNA to ribosomes, where proteins are assembled. DNA → mRNA → protein (central dogma of molecular biology).

## Photosynthesis

Photosynthesis is the process by which plants (and algae, cyanobacteria) convert light energy into chemical energy:
CO₂ + H₂O + light → glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆) + O₂

Chlorophyll in chloroplasts absorbs sunlight (mainly red and blue wavelengths). The light reactions produce ATP and NADPH; the Calvin cycle uses these to fix CO₂ into glucose.

## The Immune System and Vaccines

The immune system defends against pathogens. Two branches:
- Innate immunity: non-specific, immediate — skin, mucus, fever, phagocytes.
- Adaptive immunity: specific, learned — B cells produce antibodies; T cells destroy infected cells.

A vaccine introduces a weakened, killed, or partial form of a pathogen (or mRNA instructions to make a pathogen protein) to train the immune system without causing disease. Memory B and T cells persist, enabling a rapid response if the real pathogen appears later.

Antibodies are proteins that bind specifically to antigens (foreign molecules) on pathogens, marking them for destruction.

## Cells and Cell Division

A cell is the basic unit of life. Two types:
- Prokaryotic cells (bacteria, archaea): no membrane-bound nucleus, simpler structure.
- Eukaryotic cells (animals, plants, fungi): have a nucleus, mitochondria, endoplasmic reticulum.

Mitosis: cell division producing two identical daughter cells (growth, repair).
Meiosis: cell division producing four genetically unique cells with half the chromosome number (sexual reproduction, gametes).

Mitochondria are the "powerhouses" of the cell — they produce ATP via aerobic respiration:
C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + ATP
