# Macroeconomics and Global Economy

## GDP and National Accounts

GDP (Gross Domestic Product) is the total monetary value of all final goods and services produced within a country in a given period (usually a year). Three methods of calculation give the same result:
- Expenditure approach: GDP = C + I + G + (X − M) where C = consumption, I = investment, G = government spending, X = exports, M = imports.
- Income approach: sum of all incomes earned in production (wages, profits, rents, interest).
- Output approach: sum of value added by all industries.

GNP (Gross National Product): output produced by a country's residents wherever they are located (includes income earned abroad, excludes income earned by foreigners domestically). GDP is more commonly used.

Real GDP adjusts for inflation; nominal GDP does not. GDP per capita = GDP ÷ population — a rough measure of living standards.

GDP growth rate measures economic expansion or contraction. Two consecutive quarters of negative GDP growth = recession.

## Inflation and Deflation

Inflation is the general rise in price levels over time — money buys less. Measured by the Consumer Price Index (CPI): tracks price changes of a basket of goods and services.

Causes of inflation:
- Demand-pull: too much money chasing too few goods (economy overheating, excess consumer spending).
- Cost-push: rising production costs (oil prices, wages) passed on as higher prices.
- Built-in (wage-price spiral): workers demand higher wages → firms raise prices → repeat.
- Monetary: too much money supply growth (Friedman: "inflation is always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon").

Hyperinflation: extremely rapid inflation (>50%/month) — destroys a currency's value. Historical examples: Weimar Germany (1923), Zimbabwe (2008), Venezuela (2010s).

Deflation (falling prices): can be dangerous — consumers delay purchases expecting further falls, reducing demand, leading to recession. Japan suffered prolonged deflation in the 1990s–2000s.

Target inflation: most central banks target ~2% annual inflation as a sign of healthy demand.

## Central Banks and Monetary Policy

A central bank is a government institution that manages a country's money supply and interest rates. Examples: Federal Reserve (US), European Central Bank (ECB), Bank of England, Bank of Japan.

Tools of monetary policy:
- Interest rates: the central bank sets the benchmark interest rate (federal funds rate in the US). Raising rates makes borrowing more expensive → reduces spending → cools inflation. Cutting rates stimulates borrowing and spending.
- Open market operations: buying government bonds injects money into the economy; selling them withdraws money.
- Reserve requirements: the fraction of deposits banks must hold in reserve (less commonly used now).
- Quantitative easing (QE): large-scale asset purchases to inject money into the economy — used when interest rates are already near zero.

The Fisher equation: nominal interest rate ≈ real interest rate + inflation rate. If inflation is 3% and the real rate is 1%, the nominal rate is ~4%.

## Fiscal Policy

Fiscal policy is government use of spending and taxation to influence the economy.
- Expansionary fiscal policy: increase spending or cut taxes to stimulate demand (used in recessions). Keynesian argument: government should fill the gap in private demand.
- Contractionary fiscal policy: cut spending or raise taxes to reduce demand (used when inflation is too high).

Budget deficit: government spends more than it collects in revenue. Budget surplus: government collects more than it spends.

National debt: the accumulated total of past deficits minus surpluses. Debt-to-GDP ratio measures sustainability.

The multiplier effect: an initial injection of government spending generates more than its direct amount in economic activity — each pound/dollar spent becomes income for others who then spend again.

## International Trade and Comparative Advantage

Comparative advantage (Ricardo): a country should specialise in and export goods it can produce at a relatively lower opportunity cost, even if another country is absolutely better at everything. Trade allows both countries to consume more than without trade.

Protectionism: tariffs (taxes on imports), quotas (limits on import quantities), subsidies (government support for domestic industries). Arguments for: protect infant industries, national security, employment. Arguments against: raises prices for consumers, invites retaliation, reduces efficiency.

Free trade agreements (FTAs): reduce trade barriers between signatory countries. Examples: NAFTA/USMCA (US, Mexico, Canada), EU single market, ASEAN FTA.

Balance of payments: records all economic transactions between a country and the rest of the world. Current account (trade in goods and services, income, transfers) + capital account + financial account = 0.

Currency exchange rates: determined by supply and demand for currencies. A strong currency makes exports more expensive and imports cheaper; a weak currency does the opposite. Fixed exchange rate: pegged to another currency or gold. Floating rate: determined by market forces.

## Business Cycles and Economic Indicators

A business cycle is the periodic expansion and contraction of economic activity:
- Expansion: GDP grows, unemployment falls, consumer confidence rises.
- Peak: maximum output; inflation may be a concern.
- Contraction/Recession: GDP falls, unemployment rises.
- Trough: minimum output; recovery begins.

Leading indicators (predict future activity): stock market, building permits, new orders, consumer confidence.
Lagging indicators (confirm past trends): unemployment rate, corporate profits, interest rates.
Coincident indicators (move with the economy): GDP, personal income, industrial production.

Unemployment types:
- Frictional: between jobs (normal; workers searching for better match).
- Structural: skills mismatch due to technological change or shifts in the economy.
- Cyclical: due to recession (lack of demand); the main concern in downturns.
- Natural rate of unemployment: frictional + structural (there's always some unemployment even in a healthy economy — typically 4–6%).

## Financial Markets

Capital markets: markets for long-term debt (bonds) and equity (stocks). Money markets: short-term debt instruments (T-bills, commercial paper).

Bond: a debt instrument — the issuer borrows money and promises periodic interest payments (coupon) and return of principal at maturity. Bond prices and interest rates move inversely: when rates rise, existing bond prices fall.

Equity/stock: ownership share in a company. Shareholders receive dividends and benefit from price appreciation. Stock markets (NYSE, NASDAQ, LSE) allow trading of shares.

Market capitalisation = share price × total shares. A stock index (S&P 500, FTSE 100, Nikkei 225) tracks the performance of a selected group of stocks.

Derivatives: financial instruments whose value derives from an underlying asset (options, futures, swaps). Used for hedging risk or speculation.

## Poverty, Inequality, and Development

The Gini coefficient measures income inequality — 0 = perfect equality, 1 = all income to one person. High-inequality countries (Brazil, South Africa) have Gini coefficients around 0.55–0.65; low-inequality (Scandinavia) around 0.25–0.30.

The Human Development Index (HDI): measures development beyond income — combines GDP per capita, life expectancy, and education (mean and expected years of schooling).

Absolute poverty: living below a defined threshold (World Bank: $2.15/day at 2017 purchasing power parity). Extreme poverty has fallen dramatically since 1990, primarily due to growth in China and India, but remains widespread in sub-Saharan Africa.

The Kuznets curve hypothesis: inequality first rises then falls as countries industrialise (disputed empirically).

Market failure: situations where the market produces suboptimal outcomes — public goods (non-excludable, non-rival: national defence, public parks), externalities (pollution, vaccines), information asymmetry (used-car market, insurance), monopoly power.
