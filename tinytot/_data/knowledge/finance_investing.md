# Finance and Investment Knowledge

## Bond Prices and Interest Rates

Bond prices and interest rates move inversely and have an inverse relationship — bond prices fall and decline when interest rates rise, and decrease downward when rates go up. Conversely, when interest rates fall, bond prices rise. This inverse, downward movement is how bond markets adjust: when new bonds are issued at higher rates, existing lower-rate bonds must decrease in price to remain attractive. The bond price falls inversely to the interest rate increase. Duration measures sensitivity: longer-maturity bonds decline more steeply when rates rise.

## The Rule of 72

The Rule of 72 is a quick mental shortcut to estimate how long it takes an investment to double at a fixed annual interest rate:

Years to double ≈ 72 ÷ annual interest rate (%)

Examples:
- At 6% annual return: 72 ÷ 6 = 12 years to double
- At 8% annual return: 72 ÷ 8 = 9 years to double
- At 12% annual return: 72 ÷ 12 = 6 years to double
- At 1% annual return: 72 ÷ 1 = 72 years to double

The Rule of 72 also works for inflation: at 3% inflation, purchasing power halves in 72 ÷ 3 = 24 years.

## Compound Interest

Compound interest is interest calculated on both the principal and accumulated interest. Formula: A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt), where P = principal, r = annual rate, n = compounding periods per year, t = years.

At 10% compounded annually: $1,000 → $1,100 after 1 year → $1,210 after 2 years (interest earns interest).
At 10% simple interest: $1,000 → $1,100 after 1 year → $1,200 after 2 years (no compounding).

Warren Buffett calls compound interest "the eighth wonder of the world." Starting early is the most powerful lever — doubling investment time roughly squares the final value.

## Stock Market Basics

A stock represents ownership (equity) in a company. Stock prices fluctuate based on supply and demand, earnings reports, economic conditions, and investor sentiment.

P/E ratio (Price-to-Earnings) = stock price ÷ earnings per share. A P/E of 20 means investors pay $20 for every $1 of annual earnings. High P/E suggests growth expectations; low P/E may signal undervaluation or slow growth.

Market capitalisation = share price × total shares outstanding. Large-cap: >$10B; mid-cap: $2–10B; small-cap: <$2B.

Dividend: portion of company profits paid to shareholders, usually quarterly.

## Dollar-Cost Averaging (DCA)

Dollar-cost averaging means investing a fixed amount of money at regular intervals (e.g., $500 per month) regardless of market conditions. When prices are low, you buy more shares; when prices are high, you buy fewer. Over time, this averages out the purchase price, reducing the risk of investing a lump sum at a market peak.

DCA works well for long-term investors who cannot time the market. It enforces discipline and removes emotional decision-making.

## Inflation

Inflation is the rate at which the general price level rises, eroding purchasing power. Measured by the Consumer Price Index (CPI). Moderate inflation (~2%) is considered healthy in developed economies. Hyperinflation (e.g., Zimbabwe 2008, Venezuela) destroys a currency's value.

Central banks (e.g., the Federal Reserve, the Bank of England) raise interest rates to combat inflation — higher rates reduce borrowing, slow spending, and cool the economy.

The real return on an investment = nominal return − inflation rate. A 7% return during 3% inflation yields a real return of approximately 4%.
